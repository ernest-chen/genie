Data updated: 2024–2025 (estimates where noted)

Executive Summary

Visa and Mastercard dominate global purchase volume with scalable open-loop network models and superior margins. American Express maintains a premium, closed-loop system with rich rewards and direct customer relationships. Discover operates a hybrid model focused on the U.S., with strong cash-back value and the PULSE debit network; its pending Capital One acquisition could reshape its scale and capabilities.

Regulatory & Competitive Landscape

Merchant Economics

  • Discount fee ranges: Amex ~2.5–3.5%, Visa/Mastercard ~1.5–2.5%, Discover ~1.5–2.5%
  • Issuer-set rewards economics drive interchange pressure (esp. premium tiers)
  • Value-added services (risk, tokenization) justify network fees

Regulation & Disruption

  • Interchange scrutiny, merchant litigation, and the Credit Card Competition Act
  • EU Digital Markets Act implications for wallets and rails
  • Competition from wallets (Apple Pay, Google Pay), BNPL, crypto, and real-time rails (RTP, FedNow)

Analyst Insights

  • Network Effects: Acceptance and issuer breadth create defensibility for Visa/Mastercard; Amex competes on lifecycle value per cardholder rather than sheer volume.
  • Unit Economics: Rewards intensity correlates with interchange tiers; value-added fraud and token services increasingly subsidize issuer rewards.
  • Multi-Rail: Card networks are converging with account-to-account and real-time rails; monetization shifts from per-transaction to services and identity.
  • Geopolitics: Domestic schemes (e.g., RuPay, MIR) and UnionPay expansion influence cross-border volumes and acceptance priorities.
